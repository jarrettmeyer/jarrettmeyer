A Journey Through the Jewish Seder

Jarrett Meyer — 15 April 2019

Agenda

  • History
  • Modern Hagaddah
  • Please ask questions along the way! Interruptions mean you are paying attention.

History

  • ~15c BCE: Israelites are liberated from Egypt.
  • ~14c BCE: Joshua, successor to Moses, celebrates the Passover at Gilgal, near Jericho.

For 300 years, the Passover is not celebrated. It is believed that only the negative commandment (do not consume chametz) is observed.

History

  • 11c BCE: Samuel, in the era of Judges, institutes the practice of Passover.
  • 10c BCE: Solomon commissions the construction of the First Temple.
  • 5c BCE: The Torah is written.

For the last 1,000 years, Torah has been an oral tradition. This includes the story of creation, Noah, Abraham, Moses, the Exodus, the events at Mt. Sinai, the stories of Miriam and Aaron, and Levitical law.

History

  • 587 BCE: Nebuchanezzar II of Babylon sieges Jerusalem and destroys the First Temple.
  • 515 BCE: Construction of the Second Temple begins.

The 72 years between Temples, the time of exile, would have been when the books of Jeremiah, Lamentations, and Ezekiel were written.

Passover During the Second Temple

Animals would be purchased or brought to the Temple for slaughter. When the masses were gathered in the Temple court, they would bar the Temple gates and blow the shofar.

The kohanim would supervise the slaughter of the lambs, catching their blood in golden bowls. As far as we know, this is the only time that families performed the slaughter themselves. All other ritual sacrifices would have been performed by the kohanim.

The levites would carry the blood to the altar and pour out the blood onto the altar. The levites would join with the gathered masses to sing Hallel (praise) Psalms.

Passover During the Second Temple

Meat would be charred and passed around. It was required that all meat had to be consumed before sunrise. Bitter herbs, or maror, and matzah would be eaten.

Children were encouraged to learn about the story of Exodus during this feast.

At some point in time during the Second Temple, charoset was introduced.

Passover During the Second Temple

The Passover, Pesach, is a single day Temple ritual, requiring priests, animal sacrifice, and a blood ritual.

The Feast of Unleavened Bread, Chag Hamatzot, is a seven day practice, forbidding the consumption of chametz. Chag Hamatzot would be followed by all Jews. This includes Jews who could not afford a sacrifice and Jews who were not in Jerusalem.

Jesus of Nazareth would not have celebrated a home Passover meal. (It did not exist yet!)

History

  • 37 BCE: The reign of King Herod the Great begins.
  • 20 BCE: Herod greatly expands the role of the Second Temple (sometimes referred to as Herod's Temple). The Temple employs over 1,000 priests.
  • 4 BCE: King Herod the Great dies. He leaves his kingdom to his four children.
    • Herod Archaleus: Judea, Samaria, Idumea.
    • Herod Antipas: Galilee, Perea.
    • Philip: Batanea.
    • Salome I: Decapolis, Yavne.

History

  • 26 CE: Pontius Pilate becomes the 5th Roman Prefect of Judea.
  • 30 CE: Jesus of Nazareth is crucified.
  • 66 CE: Zealots claim Jerusalem from the Romans.
  • 70 CE: Titus sieges Jerusalem and destroys the Second Temple.

History

  • ~70 CE: Mark is written.
  • 75 - 94 CE: Josephus Flavius writes Antiquities of the Jews.
  • ~85 CE: Matthew is written.
  • ~100 CE: John is written.
  • ~110 CE: Luke-Acts is written.
  • ~200 CE: The Mishnah (oral tradition) is written.
  • ~500 CE: The Gemara (halacha and theology) is written.

The Mishnah and Gemara, when combined, is the Jewish Talmud. It is 63 tractates (over 6,200 pages!) of Jewish thought, debate, law, stories, and theology.

The Modern Hagaddah

You shall keep the feast of unleavened bread, for on this day I brought your ancestors out of the Land of Egypt. You shall observe this day throughout the generations, a remembrance of the day on which God freed you with a mighty hand.

We praise You, God, the source of all light. Let Your light shine on us all. May our hearts and our homes be made brighter by Your love and sanctified by Your presence.

Lighting the Candles

* Light the candles.

May the festival lights we now kindle inspire us to use our powers to heal and not to harm, to help and not to hinder, to bless and not to curse, and to serve the God of freedom, always.

By praising God we say that all life is sacred. In kindling these lights, we preserve life’s sanctity. With every holy light, the world is revealed in harmony. We praise You, God, Sovereign of all life, who makes our lives holy with commandments, and calls us to kindle the festival lights.

Blessing the Home

Blessed is the home that is filled with love, in which the spirit of friendship warms the heart. Blessed is the place where harmony abides.

Blessing for Those Present

May God bless you and keep you. May God look kindly upon you and ever be gracious to you. May God show you tenderness and grant you peace.

Blessing of Time

We give thanks to You, Eternal God, Creator of the universe, who has given us life, sustained us, and enabled us to reach this holy moment.

Washing of Hands

* Pour the first cup of wine.

* Wash hands in the basin.

* Eat the karpas, dipped in salt water.

We dip parsley in salt water. The green parsley reminds us of new life. The salt water reminds us of the tears of all those who came before us to make this day possible.

Breaking the Matzah

We break the matzah. It reminds us that the world is in need of repair.

This is the bread of poverty which our ancestors ate in the land of Egypt. All who are hungry, come and eat. All in need of healing, come celebrate this seder. We pray that all pain will end soon, and that all will know healing. Next year, may all God’s children be free!

The Exodus and Ten Plagues

* Tell the story of the Exodus.

* For each plague, we stick our finger in our wine, and place a dot of wine on our plate. Each drop of wine we remove from our cup decreases our joy.

  1. The making of war.
  2. The teaching of hate and violence.
  3. The destruction of the earth and its natural resources.
  4. The pollution of air and water.
  5. The neglect of human needs.
  6. The corruption of justice.
  7. The enabling of crimes of violence.
  8. The denial of rights and freedoms.
  9. The corruption of culture, knowledge, and science.
  10. The exploitation, abuse, and neglect of children.

The Symbols of Passover

  • Karpas, or fresh greens (usually parsley or romaine), reminding us of spring renewal.
  • Pesach, the paschal lamb, the sacrificial offering of the Passover story.
  • Matzah, the bread of affliction, made in haste as we fled from slavery.
  • Maror, the bitter herb (usually horseradish), recalling the bitterness of persecution.
  • Charoset, the mortar, holding together the stones.
  • Beitzah, the egg, a universal symbol of new life.
  • Miriam's cup, a cup of water, a symbol of the well that followed the Jews in the desert.
  • The orange, the voices of all those who have been silenced, including women, homosexuals, bisexuals, and all members of the LGBTQ+ community. The orange represents the fruitfulness of inclusion. The seeds must be spit out, remaining us that old ideas that must be discarded.

The Passover Feast

* Pour the second cup of wine.

Now we eat! (Finally!)

Blessing After the Meal

* Pour the third cup of wine.

* Singing of Hallel Psalms.

We praise You, Eternal God, Creator of the universe, who sustains the world with goodness, grace, and infinite mercy. Your mercy endures forever.

Remember us this day in kindness. Amen.

Visit us this day with blessing. Amen.

Preserve us this day for life. Amen.

Benediction

* Pour the fourth cup of wine.

Next year, in a world of peace, for all people!
